# Leadership, Product Thinking & Career
Books for engineers who influence people and outcomes: leading projects, running teams, making product calls, and building a career that doesn’t burn you out.

## Start here

### *The Manager’s Path* — Camille Fournier
**Tags:** `intermediate` `manager` `career` `leadership`

**Why we recommend it:** Fournier writes with the voice of someone who actually ran engineering teams. She covers the transition from individual contributor to manager, then to director-level responsibilities. The book stays concrete: one-on-ones, feedback, hiring, and building a healthy team environment. If you just got promoted, it will steady you.

**What you get:**
- A practical map of engineering management roles and expectations
- Advice on feedback, coaching, and performance conversations
- Guidance on hiring, team design, and execution cadence
- Common mistakes new managers make, with alternatives

**Good to know:** Management looks different across companies. Use this as a guide, then adapt to your org’s constraints and culture.

**Pair it with:** *Radical Candor* for direct, humane feedback practices.

### *Staff Engineer* — Will Larson
**Tags:** `intermediate` `architect` `career` `leadership`

**Why we recommend it:** Larson explains senior technical influence without managerial authority. He outlines different staff paths: tech lead, architect, solver, and right-hand partner. Engineers like this book because it makes the role legible. You get a way to plan your impact, not just hope people notice your work.

**What you get:**
- Models of staff-level work: technical strategy, alignment, execution
- Practical ways to build influence and trust across teams
- Guidance on sponsorship, mentoring, and technical leadership
- A clearer view of what “scope” means in senior roles

**Good to know:** No book can replace organizational context. Use it to pick a path, then ask leaders in your org what they reward.

**Pair it with:** *An Elegant Puzzle* for org design and leadership tradeoffs.

## Recommended

### *An Elegant Puzzle* — Will Larson
**Tags:** `intermediate` `manager` `engineering-management` `leadership`

**Why we recommend it:** Larson covers engineering leadership as a set of tradeoffs: team topology, staffing, execution, and organizational design. He brings a pragmatic tone and focuses on how things fail in real companies. If you lead teams through growth, reorgs, or painful delivery slowdowns, this book gives you patterns that won’t sound naive.

**What you get:**
- Org and team design patterns for scaling engineering groups
- Practical thinking about prioritization and execution systems
- Advice on managing risk, reliability, and quality at the org level
- A language for leadership decisions that avoids empty slogans

**Good to know:** Some chapters assume a fast-growing org. Still, the decision models apply widely when you adjust scope.

**Pair it with:** *Accelerate* to connect leadership practices with delivery outcomes.

### *Peopleware* — Tom DeMarco, Tim Lister
**Tags:** `beginner` `manager` `teams` `career`

**Why we recommend it:** DeMarco and Lister argue that team environment and focus matter as much as raw talent. They cover interruptions, motivation, hiring, and the hidden costs of busywork. Many teams still repeat the mistakes this book calls out. Read it if you want higher output with less burnout and fewer meetings.

**What you get:**
- Clear arguments for protecting focus time and deep work
- Practical advice on team culture and motivation
- Insights on hiring and team composition
- A way to talk about productivity beyond “work harder”

**Good to know:** Some examples reflect older office culture. Translate the lessons to remote and hybrid realities, then act on the principles.

**Pair it with:** *The Manager’s Path* for a modern management playbook.

### *The Mythical Man-Month* — Frederick P. Brooks Jr.
**Tags:** `beginner` `manager` `projects` `career`

**Why we recommend it:** Brooks explains why adding people to a late project often makes it later. He describes communication overhead, coordination costs, and the non-linear nature of software work. The writing feels old-school, yet the project failures feel current. It’s a short read that can save you from schedule fantasy.

**What you get:**
- A practical model of why schedule slips accelerate
- Concepts that explain coordination overhead and ramp-up costs
- Lessons on planning, estimation, and project communication
- A reality check you can cite in planning discussions

**Good to know:** It won’t teach modern tooling, and it doesn’t need to. The value comes from the project dynamics that keep repeating.

**Pair it with:** *Accelerate* for modern delivery performance measurement.

### *Inspired* — Marty Cagan
**Tags:** `intermediate` `manager` `product` `career`

**Why we recommend it:** Cagan explains how strong product teams discover what to build and why customers care. Engineers benefit because product decisions shape technical work. The book helps you avoid building features that no one uses, then blaming engineering for the waste. It pushes cross-functional collaboration in a practical way.

**What you get:**
- How product teams test ideas before full builds
- A clear view of product discovery vs delivery
- Ways engineers can contribute to product outcomes
- Language for discussing value, risk, and feasibility with stakeholders

**Good to know:** Some orgs can’t adopt the full product model quickly. Start with the discovery habits that fit your team and iterate.

**Pair it with:** *Peopleware* for team environment and execution basics.

### *Radical Candor* — Kim Scott
**Tags:** `beginner` `manager` `communication` `leadership`

**Why we recommend it:** Scott focuses on one hard skill: give direct feedback while caring about the person. She offers frameworks and examples that help you avoid two bad extremes: being cruel or being vague. The book suits leads who run code reviews, performance conversations, and conflict resolution. It will make tough talks less awkward.

**What you get:**
- A framework for feedback that stays direct and respectful
- Guidance on coaching and handling repeated problems
- Tools for building trust across teams
- A way to manage conflict without escalation theater

**Good to know:** Frameworks can become jargon. Keep it simple: clear feedback, specific examples, and follow-up actions.

**Pair it with:** *The Manager’s Path* for broader management responsibilities.

### *The Making of a Manager* — Julie Zhuo
**Tags:** `beginner` `manager` `career` `leadership`

**Why we recommend it:** Zhuo writes from a first-time manager perspective, so the advice feels relatable and practical. She covers one-on-ones, feedback, hiring, and building trust. Many new managers fear they will fail at “people stuff”. This book reduces that anxiety with clear, step-by-step habits.

**What you get:**
- A gentle path into management responsibilities
- Advice on communication, coaching, and team health
- Practical tips for hiring and onboarding
- Examples that help you avoid common early mistakes

**Good to know:** It targets newer managers, so experienced leaders may find parts basic. Still, it’s a strong book to hand to someone on their first management role.

**Pair it with:** *An Elegant Puzzle* for scaling leadership patterns.
